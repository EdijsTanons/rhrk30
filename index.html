Service above self!

Rotary is a worldwide organization that brings together business and professional leaders to provide humanitarian support, promote high ethical standards in all professions, help carry out acts of goodwill and contribute to peace in the world.

Rotary principles

To support and foster the ideal of service in the public interest as the foundation of worthy activity, especially by supporting and encouraging:

  • the development of personal contacts and using them for service;
  • high ethical standards in work and professional life;
  • recognition of the value of every useful occupation;
  • raising the prestige of every Rotarian's occupation by serving society;
  • the ideal of service in every Rotarian's personal, professional and public life;
  • the strengthening of international understanding, goodwill and peace through the worldwide fellowship of representatives of different businesses and professions united by the ideal of service.

Only four questions, yet the foundation for everything:

  • Is it the truth?
  • Is it fair to all concerned?
  • Will it build goodwill and better friendships?
  • Will it be beneficial to all concerned?

Founding

The Riga Hansa Rotary Club was founded on 17 May 1996.

The Riga Hansa Rotary Club (RHRK) is the second Rotary club in Latvia. Its first meeting home was the Hotel Latvija. At the time of its founding the club had 33 members, many of them well-known public figures. The first president was Viktors Kulbergs, president of the Latvian Chamber of Commerce and Industry and general director of Auto Riga. During the founding process, the district governor's representative was Ēvalds Ikaunieks, adviser to the Cabinet of Ministers, and the president-elect was Jānis Dripe.

We are in a joint district with Sweden and part of the Swedish clubs. The district was No. 2410; today the number has changed to 2405, in association with Rotary in Sweden. From the very beginning, our projects, meetings and participation in district events have been closely connected with the Swedish Rotary movement. This broadened our international scope and helped us become better acquainted with Rotarian traditions and aims. The restoration of the Riga club in an independent Latvian state became possible in 1992. On 5 March 1996 Rotary International accepted RHRK into the worldwide Rotary family.

A brief history

Rotary activities and sources of information

On 9 March 1998, under the leadership of the Riga Hansa Rotary Club, the Latvian Rotary monthly magazine began publication. Its editor-in-chief was club member and theologian Vilis Vārsbergs. The magazine, titled Rotkalis Latvijai, was initially published in print. Later, as communication moved into the digital environment, its role diminished, because contact now takes place through Rotary websites, WhatsApp groups and other channels. Our club has created WhatsApp announcement, greeting and information pages. It is a pleasure when, on members' name days and birthdays, these pages fill with flowers and warm wishes.

The Rotary movement in Latvia can be read about in the Latvian National Encyclopedia entry at enciklopedia.lv/skirklis/91605. RHRK members also took part in preparing this entry.

A key element of the Rotary movement is the bond and continuity between generations. The only living participant of a pre-war Rotary club in Latvia was Kārlis Skalders (1901-2001). He became the symbolic and also legal link that made it possible, after the restoration of Latvia's independence, to restore - rather than found anew - the Rotary movement in Latvia. Skalders' life story was published by Jumava under the title Laikmeta zīmē. The book connects the Rotary movement that began in free Latvia and was interrupted by the Soviet occupation. Rotary could be reborn only after the restoration of the Latvian state.

Skalders was a student of the first graduating class of the University of Latvia, a participant in the Latvian War of Independence and the author of several books on medicine. His contribution was the restoration of the Riga Rotary Club: he had become a member in 1938 and, throughout the war and Soviet occupation, had secretly preserved the registration documents of the Riga Rotary Club. When the opportunity arose to restore Rotary in Latvia, Skalders became the living link between pre-war Rotarians and the future Rotarians of Latvia in the 1990s.

The beginnings of Rotary in Latvia

From a paper by RHRK member Ēvalds Ikaunieks:

“The Riga Rotary Club was founded on 22 March 1932 in the Otto Schwarz restaurant of the Hotel de Rome. In volume 2846 of the Latvian State Historical Archives, the original charter certificate has been preserved with the signature of the then Rotary International president Clinton P. Anderson. Paul Harris' notes about his visit to Riga have also survived: 'Latvia is undoubtedly strong in its national aspirations and goals. To build their own culture, undisturbed by outside power - that is most important to them. They are ready to make any sacrifice to achieve this goal.' On 21 March 1939 the Liepāja Rotary Club was founded. Its first president was Gabriels Krišmanis, head of the Liepāja Free Port.

The work of the Rotary movement was interrupted by the invasion of Soviet troops and the occupation that followed, which used violence against free Latvia and its people, including civic organizations. During the Year of Terror, nearly half of Latvian Rotarians were arrested, deported and died. Together with the most active part of society, separated from their families and children, Rotarians endured the road of suffering to Siberia in cattle wagons.”

The Latvian Rotary movement has many significant events, but we cannot cover them all while marking RHRK's thirtieth anniversary. We therefore mention several joint events that had a major impact on the movement's development and expansion.

The first Latvian Rotary Forum in Riga, at the House of the Blackheads - 8 April 2000

At that time there were already 14 Rotary clubs - Riga, Riga Hansa, Kuldīga, Liepāja, Valmiera, Rīdzene, Talsi, Ogre, Jelgava, Riga International, Valka, Jūrmala, Tukums, Ventspils and Salaspils - as well as the youth Riga Rotaract Club. In 2000 the Gulbene Rotary Club, for which we are “godfathers,” and the Cēsis Rotary Club were also founded.

In November 2000, at the initiative of the Riga Hansa Rotary Club, members of all clubs issued an appeal to Latvia. Its closing words were: “We call on everyone who has enough common sense, moral backbone and courage to oppose corruption of every kind! Let us make it impossible in Latvia!” This appeal remains valid for Rotarians to this day. Corruption still gnaws at Latvia and, although it is decreasing, the duty and moral responsibility of every Rotarian is to place obstacles in its path and to uphold honesty, integrity and ethical conduct in all activity.

A major event in Latvian Rotary history - the centenary of Rotary, 26-27 February 2005

Every year the leadership of Rotary International changes, and each new president brings a motto, goals and appeal. Rotary International presidents' visits to Latvia and their mottos include:

  • 2009-2010 John Kenny: “The Future of Rotary is in Your Hands”
  • 2017-2018 Ian Riseley: “Making A Difference”
  • 2018-2019 Barry Rassin: “Be The Inspiration”
  • 2022-2023 Jennifer Jones: “Imagine Rotary”

The visit of Rotary International President John Kenny to Latvia from 19 to 23 March 2010 became especially warm and moving. Much of the organizational burden fell on RHRK, and therefore there are many memories.

John Kenny warned: “The Bible says that from those to whom much is given, much is expected. Rotarians are people who have been fortunate in life; therefore we must help those who are not doing so well. Rotary is interested in cooperation with other non-governmental organizations and representatives of authority, but we must be careful in choosing partners. We must not cooperate with those who want to use our name and the trust we have earned in society.” The then District 2410 Governor Leif Carlsson replied: “We can say with our hands on our hearts that absolutely all the money goes to charity projects.” RHRK members have always kept this requirement in mind: to cooperate with honest, wise and morally convincing partners.

John Kenny felt at home here - we saw it in his eyes when he met children supported by the Cēsis Rotarians. In children's eyes we must try to see our future.

100 years of the Rotary movement

Rotary philosophy and the moral foundations of the movement

A paper by RHRK member Prof. Maija Kūle: “Future challenges for Europe and Rotary's tasks” (excerpts), delivered at the 2011 Rotary Forum in Riga.

For us, Latvian Rotarians, the nearest task is to strengthen Nordic Rotarian fellowship and joint projects. At the same time we must keep the Rotary movement throughout Europe in view, because the European Union is our common home. During the visit of Rotary International President John Kenny to Riga, we paid more attention to Rotary as an international movement. Now the time has come to consider what Rotary can contribute to Europe's cohesion, mutual assistance and overcoming of crises. Rotary must affirm values, resist violent attempts to shape politics, and call governments and nations to reasonable and balanced action.

Culture, truth and solidarity in the broadest sense are at the center of Rotary thinking: the culture of business, human relations, organization and cooperation. Participation in Rotary, based on strict ethical requirements, creates trust among members and a shared positive atmosphere that cannot be fully expressed in words. No loud criticism or reproach is needed; the environment itself and the responsibility it brings educate and organize people. In Latvia it is an honor to be part of the Rotary movement and to work alongside so many outstanding personalities in their professions. In Latvia and Northern Europe, we know, Rotarians have always stood on the side of humane and solidaristic values.

Rotary is an affirmation of humanity, not of political power or of institutions. Rotary provides space for people to become involved individually through their own club, thereby joining two impulses: be yourself and be together with others. If Paul Harris initially put forward the motto “service, not self,” he later changed it to “service beyond self.”

Does what is happening in Europe today promote friendship in intercultural processes? It seems that the European Union has entered a zone of division that could threaten its continued unity. Will Europe have to return to the age-old question: what is it? Rotarians, as top-level professionals in their fields, can contribute greatly to such debates by seeking the most successful solution and arguing for it internationally, in solidarity and wisely. That would benefit everyone.

The worldwide Rotary movement can serve as a model showing how the local and the global can be connected. Rotary clubs are not national societies by their nature, yet they always feel their national attachment - their own place, whether a meeting venue, a restaurant, a city, a country or a region of the world. Selfless human internationalism stands above all.

Rotary is not only a place for organizing aid projects or managing funds obtained from abroad. It is also a place for cultivating the highest universal values of modern culture: the ability to combine excellence with humility, personal success with the ability to share it, individual self-sufficiency with openness toward fellow members, and professional specialization with the ability to perceive diversity. The spirituality carried by Rotary has not become democratic permissiveness. Rotary's ability to consolidate people does not lead to the socially unpleasant tendency to exploit them. Openness, friendship even in crisis and hardship, and international solidarity are Rotary's strength.

The breadth of the Rotary movement is reflected in its commitments: conflict resolution and peace where there is war; understanding where there is disharmony; food security where there is hunger; health protection where there is disease; education where there is illiteracy; and sustainable economic development where there is poverty.

The challenge for contemporary Europe is sustainability: the sustainability of the euro as a currency, of the European Union as a union of states, of the EU budget and more. It is in our interest as Rotarians to support this sustainability, if only to affirm the highest values of European civilization - not merely what will happen to finances, but freedom, human rights, democracy and social responsibility. The crisis that has swept Europe can also be overcome by relying on values long understood in the Rotary movement: truth, solidarity, assistance and responsibility. With such a program, Rotary covers the world's main problems and, as we can see, is able to offer people, ideas, finances and solutions.

Life stories of the Riga Hansa Rotary Club

The first decade, 1996-2006

A collective is made strong by shared work. The first ten years of the Riga Hansa Rotary Club already proved this.

We began projects both by taking over initiatives from the Riga Rotary Club and with support from foreign clubs. The Strazdumuiža Boarding School for blind children received new equipment and apparatus worth USD 35,000 in cooperation with the Skoyen Rotary Club from Norway. This was the first Matching Grant project. It proved highly successful and was later crowned, in 2000, by an investment of USD 70,000. With the support of the Hagen Rotary Club, we began participating in a Matching Grant project to establish a prosthetics workshop at the Vaivari Rehabilitation Centre, and Rotary International supported our joint project with USD 50,000. Seeing that the investment bore fruit, our German friends provided an additional donation of DM 230,000.

During these years support was given to children's homes in Lejasstrazdi, Lielvārde, Lielplatone, Krāslava, Irši, Gatarta and Riga; to schools in Pumpuri and Gaujiena; to the Riga boarding school for children with hearing impairment; and to the elderly care home in Ērgļi. Over two years, in cooperation with German clubs and the St. John's Aid organization, a soup kitchen was equipped and maintained. A new roof was placed on the school in Lielvārde; furniture, computer equipment and minibuses were purchased; kitchens and sanitary facilities were improved in schools, boarding schools and elderly care homes. Every year Christmas gifts were provided for at least two children's homes or boarding schools. All of this is our contribution.

  • Lejasstrazdi Children's Home, total investment LVL 4,234 = EUR 5,928.
  • Gaujiena Special Boarding Primary School, total RHRK investment LVL 6,336 = EUR 8,870.
  • Reflectors for schoolchildren - “Be visible and see for yourself,” total investment LVL 7,048 = EUR 9,867.

Our Rotary “daughters,” Jūlija Brauere and Elīna Bukša, received a good education and today carry Latvia's name into the world. A Matching Grant project was begun in Ķekava (USD 82,000 = EUR 75,516) with the aim of involving young people in purposeful leisure activities and teaching a humane attitude toward others. By doing good works we gained friend clubs in Germany, Sweden, Norway and England, and everywhere we also enjoyed municipal support. Yet not everything that was invested continued. For various reasons - including changes in municipal strategies, school closures and political changes in the country - some of our projects did not achieve sustainability.

The story of the women of Iļģuciems Prison and Rotary

An unusual project, “Support for the inmates of Iļģuciems Prison,” brought great satisfaction to both recipients and donors. It is difficult to be in prison and, during that time, to change - to receive educational opportunities, to gain an understanding of a meaningful life and morality, and to help others who are in hardship. Poet Maira Asare, who had once been imprisoned in Iļģuciems, said: “Here space is so inhumanly compressed and deformed that it practically no longer exists; and one feels that, because of that, time has no connection with us either. Like a terrifying heavy carousel, it turns in its own endless circle.”

Members of the Riga Hansa Rotary Club entered this space with music, poetry, philosophical thoughts, yarn for knitting and stories about people in need in India who required help. Then one could breathe again. The main thing is the feeling of a good deed, which cleanses the soul.

The large computer project

Digital support for the regions

In the summer of 2019 the Riga Hansa Rotary Club equipped rural schools with nearly 100 computer systems, bringing them from a Swedish municipality to the Latvian countryside. In the memories of Riga Hansa Rotary Club members, this project - perhaps not large, but emotionally very meaningful - remains vivid: the delivery of computer equipment to Latvian rural schools.

The “engine” and author of the idea was our club member Jānis Andersons. Thanks to his broad contacts in Scandinavia, a unique opportunity arose to obtain almost three pallets of HP Compaq DC7900 computers and monitors. A Swedish municipality had decided to replace its equipment, but the old computers were in working order, well maintained and ready to continue serving. Our club's task was to transform the potential of this “hardware” into real support. To make the computers fully ready for the learning process, the club purchased new keyboards and mice from its own funds and made the necessary updates. The young people from the Ķekava Youth Initiative Centre helped greatly, cleaning all the computers and installing the new programs.

Logistics challenges and team spirit

RHRK 30th anniversary project: Cerību spārni for the future

A new project is taking shape in 2026

Cooperation with foreign Rotary clubs continues after the completion of earlier projects, expanding support for new initiatives. At Spāre Manor, what is now becoming the largest Cerību spārni site is taking shape. There is already a day center, a group home, and there will be a senior residence - a huge complex with many buildings, a park and the manor itself. Various master classes for local residents already take place there: painting, making useful objects, concerts, and a cafe operates. The chef at the cafe is Māris, whom we congratulated when he won an international chefs' competition. There are rooms dedicated to the memory of poet Aleksandrs Čaks, a history hall and rooms to stay in. There is much already, and much more to come. It is an active community: they have made cosmetic repairs with their own strength. Of course, it is far from ideal, but everything moves forward step by step.

“Spāre Manor - the RHRK 30th anniversary project.”

We have decided to continue cooperating with the association Cerību spārni, whose work is strongly oriented toward sustainability. Branches operate in Mālpils, Bauska, Iecava and Smiltene. Together with the chair of the association we are beginning to plan which of the current needs we can help solve. We already have a EUR 2,000 donation from our Danish friend club. But there are many needs. Several historically valuable houses belong to the manor. At present the most urgent need is to purchase equipment for the Spāre Manor cafe so that it can begin operating and generate income. The workshops and the Čaks room also need appropriate equipment.

We invite our Rotarian friends to take part in the project and hope to create a new Global Grant project in the next Rotary year.
